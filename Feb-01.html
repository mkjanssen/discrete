Section 4.1 Feb. 1: Functions

Motivating Questions.

Here are some questions we'll consider.

  1. What is a function?
  2. What are some of the key terms needed for clearly describing functions?
  3. What are some examples of functions, and how do we calculate domain, codomain, image, range, etc?

Subsection 4.1.1 Outline

Today's material comes from Section 0.4 of the book. You're encouraged to read it before coming to class.

Subsubsection 4.1.1.1 Finishing Relations

Equivalence Relation.

A relation that is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive is known as a equivalence relation.

Exploration 4.1.1.

Verify that the following are equivalence relations on the indicated sets.

  1. The relation \(\mathcal{M}\) on \(\Z\) defined by \((m,n)\in \mathcal{M}\) iff \(m-n\) is evenly divisible by 3.
  2. The relation \(\mathcal{Q}\) on \(\R\) defined by \((s,t)\in \mathcal{Q}\) iff \(s-t\) is rational.
  3. Let \(X = \set{1,\ldots, 10}\text{,}\) \(X_n = \set{2n-1,2n}\) for \(n=1,2,\ldots, 5\text{.}\) Convince yourself that
    \begin{equation*} X = X_1 \cup X_2 \cup \cdots \cup X_5. \end{equation*}
    Then, define a relation \(\mathcal{R}\) on \(X\) by \((x,y)\in \mathcal{R}\) iff there exists \(i\in \set{1,2,\ldots,5}\) for which \(x,y\in X_i\text{.}\)

Subsubsection 4.1.1.2 The definition of a function

The definition of a function we supply here is slightly different than the definition given in the book, as we have the language of relations that our book does not develop.

Definition 4.1.1.

Let \(X,Y\) be nonempty sets. A function (or map, or mapping) is a relation \(f\) on \(X\times Y\) in which each element \(x\in X\) is related to exactly one element \(y\in Y\text{.}\) We write \(y = f(x)\) and \(f : X\to Y\text{.}\) We say that \(X\) is the domain of \(f\text{,}\) \(Y\) is the codomain of \(f\text{,}\) \(y\) is the image of \(x\) under \(f\text{,}\) and the set \(\ran(f) = \setof{y\in Y}{\exists x\in X, y=f(x)}\) is the range of \(f\text{.}\)

Wow, that's a lot! Let's spend some time exploring this, first with an example.

Example 4.1.2.

Consider the following relations. Are they functions? Why or why not?

  1. \(X=\set{0,1,5}, Y = \set{\clubsuit, \spadesuit, \diamondsuit, \heartsuit}\text{,}\) \(f_1 = \set{(1,\clubsuit), (0, \heartsuit), (5,\heartsuit)}\)
  2. \(X=\set{0,1,5}, Y = \set{\clubsuit, \spadesuit, \diamondsuit, \heartsuit}\text{,}\) \(f_2 = \set{(1,\clubsuit), (0, \heartsuit), (0,\diamondsuit)}\)
  3. \(X = \set{-3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3}\text{,}\) \(Y = \set{0,1,2,\ldots,9}\text{,}\) \(f_3 = \setof{(x,x^2)}{x\in X}\)

Subsubsection 4.1.1.3 Representations of Functions

Definining a function requires three elements: a domain, a codomain, and an assignment of each member of the domain to a unique member of the codomain. As your experience suggests, there are several ways to represent that assignment, such as via a “rule” (e.g., those defined by algebraic expressions, though not all algebraic expressions define functions); via a graphical relationship; via a table, or in words (e.g., the birthday function).

In this class, our domains and codomains will generally be discrete sets, such as \(\N\) or \(\Z\text{,}\) and very often they will be finite sets. This allows us to be more explicit about the assignments we make.

Example 4.1.3.

Let's represent the functions described in Example 4.1.2 in as many ways as possible.

Exploration 4.1.2.

Which of the arrow diagrams given represent functions \(A\to B\text{,}\) with \(A = \set{1,2,3,4}\) and \(B = \set{a,b,c}\text{?}\) Explain. For those that are functions, determine the range of the function.
