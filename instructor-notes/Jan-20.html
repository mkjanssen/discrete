Section 2.2 Jan. 20: Converses, Contrapositives, Predicates and Quantifiers

Motivating Questions.

Here are some questions we'll consider.

  1. How do we introduce variables into our matheamtical statements?
  2. What types of quantifiers exist in matheamtics?
  3. How do we negate quantified statements?

Subsection 2.2.1 Outline

Today's material comes from "Predicates and Quantifiers" part of Section 0.2. You're encouraged to read it before coming to class.

Subsubsection 2.2.1.1 Converse and Contrapositive

We begin with a definition.

Converse and Contrapositive.

  • The converse of an implication \(P \imp Q\) is the implication \(Q \imp P\text{.}\) The converse is NOT logically equivalent to the original implication. That is, whether the converse of an implication is true is independent of the truth of the implication.

  • The contrapositive of an implication \(P \imp Q\) is the statement \(\neg Q \imp \neg P\text{.}\) An implication and its contrapositive are logically equivalent (they are either both true or both false).

Activity 2.2.1.

Find the converse and contrapositive for each statement in Activity 2.1.4. Are your new statements true or false? Why?

When constructing proofs, it is sometimes helpful to prove the contrapositive of a conditional rather than the conditional itself. The downside of this approach is that we generally lose the causal chain; rather than explaining why statement \(Q\) follows from \(P\text{,}\) we show that \(P\) could not be true if \(Q\) were not.

I recognize that this is pretty abstract! More details when we get to chapter 3.

Subsubsection 2.2.1.2 Predicates and Quantifiers

In order to make use of the work we've done on statements to do actual mathematics, we need a way of introducing variables. A sentence with a variable is known as a predicate.

Consider the sentence "\(m\) is composite". Is this true, false, or not a statement?

What we need to do is specify a quantifier and domain of discourse for our variable. In mathematics, we deal with two quantifiers: the existential quantifier and the universal quantifier.

Universal and Existential Quantifiers.

The existential quantifier is \(\exists\) and is read “there exists” or “there is.” For example,

\begin{equation*} \exists x (x \lt 0) \end{equation*}

asserts that there is a number less than 0.

The universal quantifier is \(\forall\) and is read “for all” or “every.” For example,

\begin{equation*} \forall x (x \ge 0) \end{equation*}

asserts that every number is greater than or equal to 0.

Activity 2.2.2.

Translate into English:

  1. \(\forall x (E(x) \imp E(x +2))\text{.}\) (The statement "\(E(m)\)" means that \(m\) is even.)
  2. \(\forall x \exists y (\sin(x) = y)\text{.}\)
  3. \(\forall y \exists x (\sin(x) = y)\text{.}\)
  4. \(\forall x \forall y (x^3 = y^3 \imp x = y)\text{.}\)
Activity 2.2.3.

Determine whether the following sentences are true, false, or not statements. If the sentence is not a statement, can you modify it, e.g., by defining a domain of discourse, which makes it into a statement? Be sure that you can explain your thinking.

  1. For all integers \(x, x^3 \ge 0\text{.}\)
  2. For all integers \(y\text{,}\) \(y\) can be written as the sum of distinct powers of 2.
  3. \(\displaystyle \forall x \forall y (x-y \le 0)\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \forall x\forall y(x^2 = y^2 \imp x = y)\)

Subsubsection 2.2.1.3 Negating Quantified Statements

Much of the time, quantifiers are relatively straightforward, even implied by a precise way of speaking. One exception to this is when we seek to negate a quantified statement.

Quantifiers and Negation.

\(\neg \forall x P(x)\) is equivalent to \(\exists x \neg P(x)\text{.}\)

\(\neg \exists x P(x)\) is equivalent to \(\forall x \neg P(x) \text{.}\)
