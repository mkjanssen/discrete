Item 5.
There are \(\binom{14}{3}\) total ways to distribute the pies without restriction. There are \(\binom{4}{1}\binom{10}{3}\) ways that one or more kids get too many pies, but we are double-counting the situations in which two kids get too many pies. So we add back the number of times two kids get too many pies, \(\binom{4}{2}\cdot \binom{6}{3}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} \binom{14}{3} - \binom{4}{1}\binom{10}{3} + \binom{4}{2}\cdot \binom{6}{3}= 4. \end{equation*}
This makes sense. The only way to ensure that no one is getting more than 3 is to give 3 to 3 kids and 2 to one. There are 4 ways to choose the kids who gets 2 pies.
