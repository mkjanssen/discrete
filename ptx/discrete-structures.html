<preface xml:id="Sec-NoteToStudents">
  <title>A Note to Students</title>
  <p>
  This work was compiled with some strong opinions.
  </p>
  <p>
  The first is that, as with a sport, instrument, or nearly any other skill, you can't learn mathematics without doing mathematics. Thus, there are few worked-out examples in this book. We would rather spend a long time in productive struggle to understand an idea deeply than be spoon-fed solutions.
  </p>
  <p>
  The second is that most people's ideas of what counts as a mathematical question are far too narrow. Mathematics is not merely geometry, algebra, and calculus (maybe with a dash of statistics for good measure). Mathematics is a way of thinking; it's about abstraction, deductive reasoning, and problem-solving. This way of thinking can be applied in surprising places, and can lead to delight and wonder at the world God has made. We will explore some of these questions this semester.
  </p>
  
</preface>

          <title>Implications</title>
        
        
       <p>
         We begin with a reminder of the following definition.
       </p>
        
       <assemblage permid="Buf">
         <title>Implications</title>
           <idx><h>implication</h></idx>
           <idx><h>conditional</h></idx>
           <idx><h>antecedent</h><see>hypothesis</see></idx>
           <idx><h>consequent</h><see>conclusion</see></idx>
           <idx>hypothesis</idx>
           <idx>conclusion</idx>
   
         <p permid="QaA">
           An <term>implication</term> or <term>conditional</term>
           is a molecular statement of the form
           <me permid="NPO">
             P \to Q
           </me>
           where <m>P</m> and <m>Q</m> are statements.
           We say that
   
           <ul permid="LxL">
             <li permid="WGK">
               <m>P</m> is the <term>hypothesis</term>
               (or <term>antecedent</term>).
             </li>
   
             <li permid="iVc">
               <m>Q</m> is the <term>conclusion</term>
               (or <term>consequent</term>).
             </li>
           </ul>
         </p>
   
         <p permid="whJ">
           An implication is <em>true</em>
           provided <m>P</m> is false or <m>Q</m> is true
           (or both),
           and <em>false</em> otherwise.
           In particular, the only way for
           <m>P \to Q</m> to be false is for <m>P</m> to be true
           <em>and</em> <m>Q</m> to be false.
         </p>
       </assemblage>

          <assemblage permid="tPG">
            <title>If and only if</title>
            <idx><h>if and only if (logical connective)</h></idx>
            <idx><h>biconditional</h></idx>
            <blockquote permid="xug">
              <p permid="goR">
                <m>P \iff Q</m> is logically equivalent to <m>(P \imp Q) \wedge (Q \imp P)</m>.

              then <m>n^2</m> is even, as well as the converse:
              if <m>n^2</m> is even, then <m>n</m> is even.

        <subsubsection>
          <title>Converse and Contrapositive</title>
        
        <p>
          We begin with a definition.
        </p>
        
        <assemblage permid="NIx">
          <title>Converse and Contrapositive</title>
          <p permid="Iwb">
            <ul permid="rEU">
              <li permid="Pcl">
                <p permid="izD">
                  The
                  <term>converse</term>
                      <idx><h>converse</h></idx>
                  of an implication
                  <m>P \imp Q</m> is the implication
                  <m>Q \imp P</m>.
                  The converse is
                  NOT logically equivalent to the original implication.
                  That is, whether the converse of an implication is true is independent of the truth of the implication.
                </p>
              </li>
    
              <li permid="vju">
                <p permid="OGM">
                  The
                  <term>contrapositive</term>
                      <idx><h>contrapositive</h></idx>
                  of an implication
                  <m>P \imp Q</m> is the statement
                  <m>\neg Q \imp \neg P</m>.
                  An implication and its contrapositive are logically equivalent
                  (they are either both true or both false).
                </p>
              </li>
            </ul>
          </p>
        </assemblage>
        
        <activity>
          <statement>
            <p>
              Find the converse and contrapositive for each statement in <xref ref="act-imptf" />. Are your new statements true or false? Why?
            </p>
          </statement>
          </activity>
          
          <p>
            When constructing proofs, it is sometimes helpful to prove the contrapositive of a conditional rather than the conditional itself. The downside of this approach is that we generally lose the causal chain; rather than explaining why statement <m>Q</m> follows from <m>P</m>, we show that <m>P</m> could not be true if <m>Q</m> were not. <aside>I recognize that this is pretty abstract! More details when we get to chapter 3.</aside>
          </p>
        
        </subsubsection>
        
        <subsubsection>
          <title>Predicates and Quantifiers</title>
          
          <p>
            In order to make use of the work we've done on statements to do actual mathematics, we need a way of introducing variables.
            A sentence with a variable is known as a <term>predicate</term>.
          </p>
          
          <p>
            Consider the sentence "<m>m</m> is composite". Is this true, false, or not a statement?
          </p>
          
          <p>
            What we need to do is specify a <emph>quantifier</emph> and <emph>domain of discourse</emph> for our variable. 
            In mathematics, we deal with two quantifiers: the existential quantifier and the universal quantifier.
          </p>
          
          <assemblage permid="mlh">
            <title>Universal and Existential Quantifiers</title>
              <idx><h>quantifier</h></idx>
      
            <p permid="ARC">
              The existential quantifier is
              <m>\exists</m> and is read
              <q>there exists</q> or
              <q>there is.</q> For example,
                  <idx><h>existential quantifier</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>quantifier</h><h>there exists</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>there exists (quantifier)</h></idx>
      
              <notation>
                <usage>\exists</usage>
                <description>existential quantifier</description>
              </notation>
      
              <me permid="aeg">
                \exists x (x \lt 0)
              </me>
              asserts that there is a number less than 0.
            </p>
      
            <p permid="gYL">
              The universal quantifier is
              <m>\forall</m> and is read
              <q>for all</q> or
              <q>every.</q> For example,
                  <idx><h>universal quantifier</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>quantifier</h><h>for all</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>for all (quantifier)</h></idx>
      
              <notation>
                <usage>\forall</usage>
                <description>universal quantifier</description>
              </notation>
      
              <me permid="Glp">
                \forall x (x \ge 0)
              </me>
              asserts that every number is greater than or equal to 0.
            </p>
          </assemblage>
          
          <activity>
            <statement>
              <p permid="dSK">
                Translate into English:
        
                <ol>
                  <li><m>\forall x (E(x) \imp E(x +2))</m>. (The statement "<m>E(m)</m>" means that <m>m</m> is even.)</li>
        
                  <li><m>\forall x \exists y (\sin(x) = y)</m>.</li>
        
                  <li><m>\forall y \exists x (\sin(x) = y)</m>.</li>
        
                  <li><m>\forall x \forall y (x^3 = y^3 \imp x = y)</m>.</li>
                </ol>
              </p>
            </statement>
            </activity>
          
          
          <activity>
            <statement>
              <p>
                Determine whether the following sentences are true, false, or not statements. If the sentence is not a statement, can you modify it, e.g., by defining a domain of discourse, which makes it into a statement? Be sure that you can explain your thinking.
              </p>
              <ol>
                <li>For all integers <m>x, x^3 \ge 0</m>.</li>
                <li>For all integers <m>y</m>, <m>y</m> can be written as the sum of distinct powers of 2.</li>
                <li><m>\forall x \forall y (x-y \le 0)</m></li>
                <li><m>\forall x\forall y(x^2 = y^2 \imp x = y)</m></li>
              </ol>
            </statement>
            </activity>
          
                      
        </subsubsection>
        
        
        <subsubsection>
          <title>Negating Quantified Statements</title>
          
          
          <p>
            Much of the time, quantifiers are relatively straightforward, even implied by a precise way of speaking.
            One exception to this is when we seek to negate a quantified statement.
          </p>
          
          <assemblage permid="Ssq">
            <title>Quantifiers and Negation</title>
            <idx><h>quantifier</h><h>interaction with negation</h></idx>

              <p permid="Mwa">

                <m>\exists x \neg P(x)</m>.

                <m>\forall x \neg P(x)

            <definition>

                  Let <m>X,Y</m> be nonempty sets.

          <subsubsection>
            <title>Recursive Definitions of Functions</title>
            
            <p>
              We've so far seen that we can define functions by listing ordered pairs, giving rules, drawing arrow diagrams, and the like. Another important method for defining functions (especially in computer science) is with a <term>recursive definition</term>.
            </p>
            
            <p permid="DSZ">
              Recursively defined functions are often easier to create from a
              <q>real world</q> problem,
              because they describe how the values of the functions are changing.
              However, this comes with a price.
              It is harder to calculate the image of a single input,
              since you need to know the images of other (previous) elements in the domain.
            </p>
        
            <assemblage permid="aNA">
              <title>Recursively Defined Functions</title>
                <idx><h>recursively defined function</h></idx>
        
              <p permid="RmK">
                <idx><h>initial condition</h><h>for a function</h></idx>
                <idx><h>recurrence relation</h><h>for a function</h></idx>
                For a function <m>f:\N \to \N</m>,
                a <term>recursive definition</term>
                consists of an <term>initial condition</term>
                together with a <term>recurrence relation</term>.
                The initial condition is the explicitly given value of <m>f(0)</m>.
                The recurrence relation is a formula for <m>f(n+1)</m> in terms for <m>f(n)</m>
                (and possibly <m>n</m> itself).
              </p>
            </assemblage>
            
            <exploration>

                </p>

            <title>Image</title>
            
            <p>
              Let <m>f : X\to Y</m> be a function. The <term>image of <m>x\in X</m> under <m>f</m></term> is the value <m>y\in Y</m> for which <m>f(x) = y</m>.
              This extends to entire subsets of the domain. Given <m>A\subseteq X</m>, the <term>image of <m>A</m> under <m>f</m></term> is the set 
              <me>
                f(A) = \setof{f(x)}{x\in A}.
              </me>
            </p>

          <subsubsection>
            <title>Inverse Images (Preimages)</title>
          
            <p>
              If images tell us where functions send elements and sets of the codomain, then <term>inverse images</term> (sometimes called <em>preimages</em>) describe which objects in the domain get sent to particular objects (elements, subsets) of the codomain. <aside>I differ from our book in preferring the term <q>preimage</q>; the inverse image of sets and elements in the codomain can be calculated even for functions which do not have inverses, and I find the double meaning of the word <q>inverse</q> annoying.</aside>
            </p>
            
            <p>
              Given a subset <m>B\subseteq Y</m>, the <term>inverse image of <m>B</m> under <m>f</m></term> the set 
              <me>
                f^{-1}(B) = \setof{x\in X}{f(x)\in B}.
              </me>
              If <m>B = \set{y}</m> for some <m>y\in Y</m>, we often write <m>f^{-1}(\set{y})</m> as <m>f^{-1}(y)</m>; it is the <term>complete inverse image of <m>y</m> under <m>f</m></term> (but again, <m>f^{-1}</m> need not be a well-defined function).
            </p>

              <title>Cardinality of a union (3 sets)</title>
              <idx>
                <h>principle of inclusion/exclusion</h>
                <h>for 3 sets</h>
              </idx>
              <idx>
                <h>cardinality</h>
                <h>of a union</h>
              </idx>
              <p permid="dOx">
                For any finite sets <m>A</m>, <m>B</m>, and <m>C</m>,
                <me permid="mcQ">
                  \card{A \cup B \cup C} = \card{A} + \card{B} + \card{C} - \card{A \cap B} - \card{A \cap C} - \card{B \cap C} + \card{A \cap B \cap C}
                </me>.
              </p>
            </assemblage>
        
            <p permid="cJe">
              To determine how many elements are in at least one of <m>A</m>,
              <m>B</m>,
              or <m>C</m> we add up all the elements in each of those sets.
              However, when we do that,
              any element in both <m>A</m> and <m>B</m> is counted twice.
              Also, each element in both <m>A</m> and <m>C</m> is counted twice,
              as are elements in <m>B</m> and <m>C</m>,
              so we take each of those out of our sum once.
              But now what about the elements which are in <m>A \cap B \cap C</m> (in all three sets)?
              We added them in three times,
              but also removed them three times.
              They have not yet been counted.
              Thus we add those elements back in at the end.
            </p>
            
            
            <p permid="VeF">
              This process of adding in, then taking out, then adding back in,
              and so on is called the <em>Principle of Inclusion/Exclusion</em>,
              or simply PIE. We will return to this counting technique later to solve for more complicated problems
              (involving more than 3 sets).
            </p>
          
          </subsubsection>
          
          
          <subsubsection>

      <subsubsection>
            <title>Permutations</title>
            
            <investigation permid="bpD">
              <statement>
              <p permid="ltj">
                You have a bunch of chips which come in five different colors:
                red, blue, green, purple and yellow.
        
                <ol permid="YuX">
                  <li permid="ECg">
                    <p permid="BHA">
                      How many different two-chip stacks can you make if the bottom chip must be red or blue?
                      Explain your answer using both the additive and multiplicative principles.
                    </p>
                  </li>
        
                  <li permid="kJp">
                    <p permid="hOJ">
                      How many different three-chip stacks can you make if the bottom chip must be red or blue and the top chip must be green,
                      purple or yellow?
                      How does this problem relate to the previous one?
                    </p>
                  </li>
        
                  <li permid="QQy">
                    <p permid="NVS">
                      How many different three-chip stacks are there in which no color is repeated?
                      What about four-chip stacks?
                    </p>
                  </li>
        
                  <li permid="wXH">
                    <p permid="udb">
                      Suppose you wanted to take three different colored chips and put them in your pocket.
                      How many different choices do you have?
                      What if you wanted four different colored chips?
                      How do these problems relate to the previous one?
                    </p>
                  </li>
                </ol>
              </p>
              </statement>
              <solution>
              
                <ol>
                    <li>If the bottom chip must be red or blue, there are two choices there. Then there are five choices for the next (top) chip, so <m>2\cdot 5 = 10</m>.</li>
                    <li>For each of the 10 stacks from the previous part, there are three choices for the new top chip, so <m>3\cdot 10 = 30</m> choices in total.</li>
                    <li><p>I read the problem with order mattering, e.g., RBY is a different stack than YBR. First, choose the three distinct colors from the five in <m>\binom{5}{3}</m> ways. For each choice of three colors, there are <m>3\cdot 2\cdot 2 = 6</m> ways to order them. This leaves 60 total stacks.</p>
                    <p>If we're building a stack of four chips, there are 5 choices of which chip to leave out and <m>4!</m> ways to order the colors once they're selected, for <m>5! = 120</m> orderings.</p></li>
                    <li>If we only want to choose the colors, there are <m>\binom{5}{3}</m> and <m>\binom{5}{4}</m> ways to do so.</li>
                </ol>


            </solution>

              as seen in the previous example is <m>6! = 6\cdot 5 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1</m>.

              </p>

                </p>

              <solution>
                <p>
                    There are <m>6^6</m> total functions: six choices for each of the six elements of the domain.
                    However, note that a bijection corresponds to a permutation of the letters a through f. There are <m>6!</m> such orderings and thus <m>45,936</m> non-bijective functions.
                </p>

              </solution>

              </exploration>

                <statement>
                  <p>
                    Consider the set <m>A = \set{a,b,c,d,e,f,g,h}</m>.
                  </p>

                    <li>How many ways are there to permute all the elements of <m>A</m>?</li>
                    <li>How many 7-letter <q>words</q> (that is, strings of letters) can you make from the elements of <m>A</m>? Assume no repeated letters.</li>
                    <li>How many 6-letter words can you make from the elements of <m>A</m>? Assume no repeated letters.</li>
                    <li>Suppose now that <m>X</m> is a set of cardinality <m>n</m>. How many ways are there to order <m>k\le n</m> distinct objects from <m>X</m>? Conjecture a formula.</li>

                  </ol>

                    <ol>
                        <li>There are <m>7!</m> such permutations.</li>
                        <li>It depends: if letters cannot be repeated, then this is the same as the previous question.
                        
                        If letters <em>can</em> be repeated, it's a much more complicated question.</li>
                        <li>First, we choose the six letters we'll use: there are <m>\binom{7}{6}</m> ways to do that. Then there are <m>6!</m> ways to arrange them, for a total of <m>7!</m> six-letter words.</li>
                        <li>First, we choose the <m>\binom{n}{k}</m> objects. Then we order them <m>k!</m> ways, for a total of <m>\binom{n}{k}\cdot k!</m> possible orderings of <m>k</m> objects chosen from <m>n</m> distinct objects.</li>
                        <li>This is the same question as the previous one!</li>
                    </ol>

                </solution>

                </exploration>
                
                <assemblage permid="Krs">
                  <title><m>k</m>-permutations of <m>n</m> elements</title>
                  <idx><h sortby="k-permutation of n
                      elements"><m>k</m>-permutation of <m>n</m>
                  elements</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>permutation</h></idx>
                  
                  <p permid="xHB">
                    <m>P(n,k)</m> is the number of <term><m>k</m>-permutations of <m>n</m> elements</term>,
                    the number of ways to <em>arrange</em>
                    <m>k</m> objects chosen from <m>n</m> distinct objects.
                    <me permid="knQ">
                      P(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} = n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots (n-(k-1))
                    </me>.
                  </p>
                </assemblage>
                
                
                <p permid="Hbn">
                  Here is another way to find the number of <m>k</m>-permutations of <m>n</m> elements:
                  first select which <m>k</m> elements will be in the permutation,
                  then count how many ways there are to arrange them.
                  Once you have selected the <m>k</m> objects,
                  we know there are <m>k!</m> ways to arrange (permute) them.
                  But how do you select <m>k</m> objects from the <m>n</m>?
                  You have <m>n</m> objects, and you need to
                  <em>choose</em> <m>k</m> of them.
                  You can do that in <m>{n \choose k}</m> ways.
                  Then for each choice of those <m>k</m> elements,
                  we can permute <em>them</em> in <m>k!</m> ways.
                  Using the multiplicative principle,
                  we get another formula for <m>P(n,k)</m>:
                  <me permid="QuZ">
                    P(n,k) = {n \choose k}\cdot k!
                  </me>.
                </p>
            
                <p permid="niw">
                  Now since we have a closed formula for <m>P(n,k)</m> already,
                  we can substitute that in:
                  <me permid="wCi">
                    \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} = {n \choose k} \cdot k!
                  </me>.
                </p>
            
                <p permid="TpF">
                  If we divide both sides by <m>k!</m> we get a closed formula for <m>{n \choose k}</m>.
                </p>
            
                <assemblage permid="qyB">
                  <title>Closed formula for <m>{n \choose k}</m></title>
                  <idx><h>binomial coefficient</h><h>closed formula for</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>combination</h><h>closed formula for</h></idx>
                  <p permid="dOK">
                    <me permid="cJr">
                      {n \choose k} = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!} = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-(k-1))}{k(k-1)(k-2)\cdots 1}
                    </me>.
                  </p>
                </assemblage>
            
                <p permid="zwO">
                  <idx><h>combination</h><h>vs permutation</h></idx>
                  <idx><h>permutation</h><h>vs combination</h></idx>
                  We say <m>P(n,k)</m> counts <em>permutations</em>,
                  and <m>{n \choose k}</m> counts <em>combinations</em>.
                  The formulas for each are very similar,
                  there is just an extra <m>k!</m> in the denominator of <m>{n \choose k}</m>.
                  That extra <m>k!</m> accounts for the fact that
                  <m>{n \choose k}</m> does not distinguish between the different orders that the <m>k</m> objects can appear in.
                  We are just selecting
                  (or choosing)
                  the <m>k</m> objects, not arranging them.
                  Perhaps <q>combination</q> is a misleading label.
                  We don't mean it like a combination lock
                  (where the order would definitely matter).
                  Perhaps a better metaphor is a combination of flavors
                  <mdash/> you just need to decide which flavors to combine,
                  not the order in which to combine them.
                </p>

                    </p>

                      <li>Three choices for sauce (red, white, BBQ)</li>
                      <li>Fifteen choices for toppings</li>
                    </ul>
                    <p>
                      How many different pizzas are possible if Prof. Clark can make one choice for crust, one choice for sauce, and then choose any number of toppings?
                    </p>
                  </statement>
                  <solution>
                    <p>

                        We will assume no repeated toppings (or at least that, e.g., double pepperoni is the same as single pepperoni for the purposes of counting combinations of toppings).

                        </me>

                    </p>

            <example permid="LpH">
              <statement>
                <p permid="KyJ">
                  Give an algebraic proof for the binomial identity
                  <me permid="yUl">
                    {n \choose k} = {n-1\choose k-1} + {n-1 \choose k}
                  </me>.
                </p>
              </statement>
              <solution permid="AQC">
        
                <proof permid="gXL">
                  <p permid="vLk">
                    By the definition of <m>{n \choose k}</m>, we have
                    <me permid="fbu">
                      {n-1 \choose k-1} = \frac{(n-1)!}{(n-1-(k-1))!(k-1)!} = \frac{(n-1)!}{(n-k)!(k-1)!}
                    </me>
                    and
                    <me permid="LiD">
                      {n-1 \choose k} = \frac{(n-1)!}{(n-1-k)!k!}
                    </me>.
                  </p>
        
                  <p permid="bSt">
                    Thus, starting with the right-hand side of the equation:
                    <md permid="Ftr">
                      <mrow>{n-1 \choose k-1} + {n-1 \choose k} \amp = \frac{(n-1)!}{(n-k)!(k-1)!}+ \frac{(n-1)!}{(n-1-k)!\,k!}</mrow>
                      <mrow>\amp = \frac{(n-1)!k}{(n-k)!\,k!} + \frac{(n-1)!(n-k)}{(n-k)!\,k!}</mrow>
                      <mrow>\amp = \frac{(n-1)!(k+n-k)}{(n-k)!\,k!}</mrow>
                      <mrow>\amp = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!\, k!}</mrow>
                      <mrow>\amp = {n \choose k}</mrow>
                    </md>.
                  </p>
        
                  <p permid="HZC">
                    The second line
                    (where the common denominator is found)
                    works because <m>k(k-1)! = k!</m> and <m>(n-k)(n-k-1)! = (n-k)!</m>.
                  </p>
                </proof>
        
              </solution>
            </example>

            <title>Stars and Bars Charts</title>
            
            
            <p permid="VWd">
              Consider the following counting problem:
            </p>
        
            <blockquote permid="zEf">
              <p permid="EXS">
                You have 7 cookies to give to 4 kids.
                How many ways can you do this?
              </p>
            </blockquote>
        
            <p permid="Cdm">
              Take a moment to think about how you might solve this problem.
              You may assume that it is acceptable to give a kid no cookies.
              Also, the cookies are all identical and the order in which you give out the cookies does not matter.
            </p>
            
            
            <p permid="GNf">
              
              All we really need to do is say when to switch from one kid to the next.
              In terms of cookies,
              we need to say after how many cookies do we stop giving cookies to the first kid and start giving
              cookies to the second kid.
              And then after how many do we switch to the third kid?
              And after how many do we switch to the fourth?
              So the way we'll represent an outcome is like this:
              <me permid="UQK">
                ***|*|*|**
              </me>.
            </p>
        
            <p permid="mUo">
              Three cookies go to the first kid,
              then we switch and give one cookie to the second kid, then switch,
              one to the third kid, switch, two to the fourth kid.
              Notice that we need 7 stars and 3 bars
              <ndash/>
              one star for each cookie,
              and one bar for each switch between kids,
              so one fewer bars than there are kids
              (we don't need to switch after the last kid
              <ndash/>
              we are done).
            </p>
        
            <p permid="Tbx">
              Why have we done all of this?
              Simple: to count the number of ways to distribute 7 cookies to 4 kids,
              all we need to do is count how many
              <em>stars and bars</em>
              charts there are.
              But a <term>stars and bars chart</term>
              <idx><h>stars and bars</h><h>chart</h></idx>
              is just a string of symbols, some stars and some bars.
              If instead of stars and bars we would use 0's and 1's, it would just be a bit string.
              <idx><h>bit string</h><h>relation to stars and bars</h></idx>
              We know how to count those.
              First, let's make sure we really know what we're doing.
            </p>

<example permid="Agv">
  <statement>
    <p permid="oQs">
      How many derangements are there of 4 elements?
    </p>
  </statement>
  <solution permid="GFB">
    <p permid="Ckd">
      We count all permutations, and subtract those which are not derangements.
      There are <m>4! = 24</m> permutations of 4 elements.
      Now for a permutation to <em>not</em> be a derangement,
      at least one of the 4 elements must be fixed.
      There are <m>{4 \choose 1}</m> choices for which single element we fix.
      Once fixed, we need to find a permutation of the other three elements.
      There are <m>3!</m> permutations on 3 elements.
    </p>
    <p permid="irm">
      But now we have counted too many non-derangements,
      so we must subtract those permutations which fix two elements.
      There are <m>{4 \choose 2}</m> choices for which two elements we fix,
      and then for each pair, <m>2!</m> permutations of the remaining elements.
      But this subtracts too many,
      so add back in permutations which fix 3 elements,
      all <m>{4 \choose 3}1!</m> of them.
      Finally subtract the <m>{4 \choose 4}0!</m> permutations
      (recall <m>0! = 1</m>)
      which fix all four elements.
      All together we get that the number of derangements of 4 elements is:
      <me permid="aro">
        4! - \left[{4 \choose 1}3! - {4 \choose 2}2! + {4 \choose 3} 1! - {4 \choose 4}0!\right] = 24 - 15 = 9
      </me>.
    </p>
  </solution>
</example>

      <idx>surjection</idx>

          How many of the functions <m>f: \{1,2,3,4,5\} \to \{1,2,3,4,5\}</m> are surjective?

        </p>

<example permid="kXx">
  <idx><h>surjection</h><h>counting</h></idx>
  <statement>
    <p permid="tAl">
      <ol permid="IuO">
        <li permid="EuP">
          <p permid="PDO">
            How many functions <m>f: \{1,2,3,4,5\} \to \{a,b,c,d\}</m> are surjective?
          </p>
        </li>

        <li permid="kBY">
          <p permid="vKX">
            How many functions <m>f: \{1,2,3,4,5\} \to \{a,b,c,d,e\}</m> are surjective?
          </p>
        </li>
      </ol>
    </p>
  </statement>
  <solution permid="Lpu">
    <p permid="LDP">
      <ol permid="wgw">
        <li permid="QJh">
          <p permid="bSg">
            There are <m>4^5</m> functions all together;
            we will subtract the functions which are not surjective.
            We could exclude any one of the four elements of the codomain,
            and doing so will leave us with <m>3^5</m> functions for each excluded element.
            This counts too many so we subtract the functions which exclude two of the four elements of the codomain,
            each pair giving <m>2^5</m> functions.
            But this excludes too many,
            so we add back in the functions which exclude three of the four elements of the codomain,
            each triple giving <m>1^5</m> function.
            There are <m>{4 \choose 1}</m> groups of functions excluding a single element,
            <m>{4 \choose 2}</m> groups of functions excluding a pair of elements,
            and <m>{4 \choose 3}</m> groups of functions excluding a triple of elements.
            This means that the number of functions which are
            <em>not</em> surjective is:
            <me permid="Liq">
              {4 \choose 1}3^5 - {4 \choose 2}2^5 + {4 \choose 3}1^5
            </me>.
            We can now say that the number of functions which are surjective is:
            <me permid="rpz">
              4^5 - \left[{4 \choose 1}3^5 - {4 \choose 2}2^5 + {4 \choose 3}1^5\right]
            </me>.
          </p>
        </li>

        <li permid="wQq">
          <p permid="HZp">
            The number of surjective functions is:
            <me permid="XwI">
              5^5 - \left[{5 \choose 1}4^5 - {5 \choose 2}3^5 + {5 \choose 3}2^5 - {5 \choose 4}1^5\right]
            </me>.
            We took the total number of functions <m>5^5</m> and subtracted all that were not surjective.
            There were <m>{5 \choose 1}</m> ways to select a single element from the codomain to exclude from the range,
            and for each there were <m>4^5</m> functions.
            But this double counts,
            so we use PIE and subtract functions excluding two elements from the range:
            there are <m>{5 \choose 2}</m> choices for the two elements to exclude,
            and for each pair, <m>3^5</m> functions.
            This takes out too many functions,
            so we add back in functions which exclude 3 elements from the range:
            <m>{5 \choose 3}</m> choices for which three to exclude,
            and then <m>2^5</m> functions for each choice of elements.
            Finally we take back out the 1 function which excludes 4 elements for each of the
            <m>{5 \choose 4}</m> choices of 4 elements.
          </p>

          <p permid="ogy">
            If you happen to calculate this number precisely,
            you will get 120 surjections.
            That happens to also be the value of <m>5!</m>.
            This might seem like an amazing coincidence until you realize that every surjective function <m>f:X \to Y</m> with
            <m>\card{X} = \card{Y}</m> finite must necessarily be a bijection.
            The number of bijections is always <m>\card{X}!</m> in this case.
            What we have here is a <em>combinatorial proof</em>
            of the following identity:
            <me permid="DDR">
              n^n - \left[{n\choose 1}(n-1)^n - {n \choose 2}(n-2)^n + \cdots + {n \choose n-1}1^n \right] = n!
            </me>.
          </p>
        </li>
      </ol>
    </p>
  </solution>
</example>
