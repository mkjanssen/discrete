Subsubsection 4.2.1.1 Reading Discussion

Discussion 4.2.1.

We'll let the discussion take us where it may, but here are some suggested talking points to get started.

  • What is matheamtics? What connection do you see between doing mathematics and being human?
  • Think of a time you were captivated by exploring something. What analogies can you draw between doing math and doing this exploration?
  • How does abstraction enrich the meaning of an idea? Give an example from your own experiences.
  • Think of an activity you associate with play. Make a list of all the things you enjoy about its playful aspects. Does your list have analogies in mathematical activities?
  • What role does beauty have in the Christian life?
  • Where is mathematical beauty found in the world?

Subsubsection 4.2.1.2 More Function Examples

Exploration 4.2.1.

Let \(b\) be the function that assigns to each person his or her birthday (month and day).

  1. Explain why \(b\) really is a function. We'll call it the birthday function. What are the domain and codomain of \(b\text{?}\)
  2. What is \(\ran(b)\text{?}\)
  3. Emmy Noether was one of the founders of abstract algebra, and also made significant contributions to mathematical physics via her discovery of Noether's Theorem. Her birthday was March 23, 1882. Translate this into a function statement, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} b(??) = ?? \end{equation*}
  4. Is the following statement true or false? Explain.
    For each day \(D\) of the year, there exists a person \(x\) such that \(b(x)=D\text{.}\)
  5. Is the following statement true or false? Explain.
    For any people \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are different people, then \(b(x) \ne b(y)\text{.}\)
Exploration 4.2.2.

Let \(\sigma\) be the function that associates to each positive natural number the sum of its distinct natural number divisors. This is called the sum of the divisors function. For example, the natural number divisors of 6 are 1, 2, 3, and 6. Thus:

\begin{equation*} \sigma(6) = 1 + 2 + 3 +6 = 12. \end{equation*}
  1. Calculate \(\sigma(k)\) for \(k = 1, 2, \ldots, 10\text{.}\)
  2. Does there exist a natural number \(n\) such that \(\sigma(n) = 5\text{?}\) Justify your conclusion.
  3. Is it possible to find two different natural numbers \(m \ne n\) such that \(\sigma(m) = \sigma(n)\text{?}\) Explain.

Subsubsection 4.2.1.3 Injective and Surjective Functions

Two important properties functions may possess are that of injectivity and surjectivity.

Injective, Surjective, and Bijective Functions.

A function is injective provided every element of the codomain is the image of at most one element from the domain. An injective function is often called an injection.

A function is surjective provided every element of the codomain is the image of at least one element from the domain. A surjective function is often called a surjection.

A function that is both injective and surjective is called bijective, and referred to as a bijection.
