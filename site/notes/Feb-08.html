Section 5.1 Feb. 8: The Additive and Multiplicative Principles

Motivating Questions.

Here are some questions we'll consider.

  1. What are counting problems?
  2. What principles can we develop to explore introductory counting problems?
  3. (If time): How can we use the language of sets to aid in counting?

Subsection 5.1.1 Outline

Today's material comes from the first part of Section 1.1 of the book. You're encouraged to read it before coming to class.

Subsubsection 5.1.1.1 First Principles

Today we start counting. Well, you've been doing a form of counting your whole life, and we saw an initial example of the types of counting questions in which we'll be interested in Exploration 4.3.2. Our counting questions will often take the form “How many possible qrsabs are there subject to the constraint jkasdghjkl, where the order of the qrsabs may/may not matter?” (And where these words are mostly made up).

Consider the following investigation from our book.

Investigation 5.1.1.

  1. A restaurant offers 8 appetizers and 14 entrées. How many choices do you have if:

    1. you will eat one dish, either an appetizer or an entrée?
    2. you are extra hungry and want to eat both an appetizer and an entrée?

  2. Think about the methods you used to solve question 1. Write down the rules for these methods.

  3. Do your rules work? A standard deck of playing cards has 26 red cards and 12 face cards.

    1. How many ways can you select a card which is either red or a face card?
    2. How many ways can you select a card which is both red and a face card?
    3. How many ways can you select two cards so that the first one is red and the second one is a face card?

This investigation suggests the following principle.

Additive Principle.

The additive principle states that if event \(A\) can occur in \(m\) ways, and event \(B\) can occur in \(n\) disjoint ways, then the event “\(A\) or \(B\)” can occur in \(m + n\) ways.

Question 5.1.1.

What does the word disjoint mean in the context of the additive principle? What if we have three different events?

Subsubsection 5.1.1.2 The Multiplicative Principle

Let's consider the following investigation.

Investigation 5.1.2.

Prof. Clark is getting ready to order pizzas for the next Pizza and Presentation. Suppose there are:

  • Three choices for crusts (thin, hand-tossed, thick)
  • Three choices for sauce (red, white, BBQ)
  • Fifteen choices for toppings
  1. How many different pizzas are possible if Prof. Clark insists on a thin crust with red sauce and one topping?
  2. Suppose now that, while insisting on thin crust and a single topping, Prof. Clark is considering any choice of sauce. How many different such pizzas are possible?
  3. Explain why your answer to the previous question makes sense in terms of the addition principle above. Is there another general rule or principle that we can articulate?
  4. Given your answer to the previous question, how many different single-topping pizzas could Prof. Clark order? How many two-topping pizzas could he order?
Multiplicative Principle.

The multiplicative principle states that if event \(A\) can occur in \(m\) ways, and each possibility for \(A\) allows for exactly \(n\) ways for event \(B\text{,}\) then the event “\(A\) and \(B\)” can occur in \(m \cdot n\) ways.

Exploration 5.1.3.

We usually write numbers in decimal form (or base 10), meaning numbers are composed using 10 different “digits” \(\{0,1,\ldots, 9\}\text{.}\) Sometimes though it is useful to write numbers hexadecimal or base 16. Now there are 16 distinct digits that can be used to form numbers: \(\{0, 1, \ldots, 9, \mathrm{A, B, C, D, E, F}\}\text{.}\) So for example, a 3 digit hexadecimal number might be 2B8.

  1. How many 2-digit hexadecimals are there in which the first digit is E or F? Explain your answer in terms of the additive principle (using either events or sets).

  2. Explain why your answer to the previous part is correct in terms of the multiplicative principle (using either events or sets). Why do both the additive and multiplicative principles give you the same answer?

  3. How many 3-digit hexadecimals start with a letter (A-F) and end with a numeral (0-9)? Explain.

  4. How many 3-digit hexadecimals start with a letter (A-F) or end with a numeral (0-9) (or both)? Explain.

Subsubsection 5.1.1.3 Counting with Sets

One of our uses for the language of set theory we developed a few weeks ago is to make our counting arguments unambiguous and rigorous.

Additive Principle (with sets).

Given two sets \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) if \(A \cap B = \emptyset\) (that is, if there is no element in common to both \(A\) and \(B\)), then

\begin{equation*} \card{A \cup B} = \card{A} + \card{B}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Similarly, we obtain a set-theoretic version of the multiplicative principle.

Multiplicative Principle (with sets).

Given two sets \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) we have \(\card{A \times B} = \card{A} \cdot \card{B}\text{.}\)

Subsubsection 5.1.1.4 Exploring PIE

We close today by thinking about the following question.

Question 5.1.2.

Suppose Casey's Bakery produces some number of pies: 10 contain blueberries, and 15 contain peaches.

  1. What is the largest number of pies Casey's could have produced?
  2. What is the smallest number of pies Casey's could have produced?
  3. What information do we need in order to determine the precise number of pies that Casey's produced?
  4. Present your findings in terms of the cardinalities of certain sets.
