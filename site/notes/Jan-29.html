Section 3.3 Jan. 29: Relations

Motivating Questions.

Here are some questions we'll consider.

  1. What are some more (non)examples of equivalence relations?
  2. How do we verify whether a given relation is an equivalence relation or not?
  3. What are partitions? What do they have to do with equivalence relations?

Subsection 3.3.1 Outline

Today's material is not in the book. For more on relations, feel free to consult Sundstrom's Mathematical Reasoning book.

Subsubsection 3.3.1.1 Cartesian Products

Cartesian Product.

The last way we'll combine sets \(A\) and \(B\) involves making ordered pairs. We say the Cartesian product of \(A\) with \(B\) is the set of all ordered pairs \((a,b)\) in which \(a\in A\) and \(b\in B\text{.}\) That is, \(A \times B = \{(a,b) \st a \in A \wedge b \in B\}\text{.}\)

Activity 3.3.1.

Let \(A = \set{\square,\triangle,\bigcirc}\) and \(B=\set{2,3}\text{.}\) List the elements of either \(A\times B\) or \(B\times A\) (your choice). Do the two products have the same elements? Explain. Then calculate \(\card{A\times A}\text{.}\)

Subsubsection 3.3.1.2 Relations

Today's material is not in our book. For more on relations, feel free to consult Sundstrom's Mathematical Reasoning book.

Relations.

Let \(S,T\) be sets. A relation\(\mathcal{R}\) on \(S\) and \(T\) is a subset of \(S\times T\text{.}\) That is, \(\mathcal{R}\subseteq S\times T\text{.}\) We write \((s,t)\in \mathcal{R}\) to say that \(s\) is related to \(t\) under the relation. We sometimes write \(s \mathcal{R} t\text{.}\) The relation class of \(x\in S\) is the set of everything related to \(x\) from \(T\text{:}\) \([x] = \setof{y\in T}{x\mathcal{R}y}\text{.}\)

Exploration 3.3.2.

Define a relation \(\mathcal{D}\) on \(\Z\) by \((m,n)\in \mathcal{D}\) iff \(m\) divides \(n\text{.}\) Calculate:

  1. \(\displaystyle [6]\)
  2. \(\displaystyle [10]\)
  3. \(\displaystyle [30]\)
  4. \(\displaystyle [60]\)
  5. \(\displaystyle [6]\cap[10]\)
Properties of Relations.

Let \(X\) be a set and \(\sim\) a relation on \(X\text{,}\) so that if \(x,y\in X\) and \((x,y)\in \sim\text{,}\) we may write the more conventional \(x\sim y\text{.}\)

  1. We say that \(\sim\) is reflexive if whenever \(x\in X\text{,}\) \(x\sim x\text{.}\)
  2. We say that \(\sim\) is symmetric if whenever \(x,y\in X\) and \(x\sim y\text{,}\) then \(y\sim x\text{.}\)
  3. We say that \(\sim\) is transitive if whenever \(x,y,z\in X\) such that \(x\sim y\) and \(y\sim z\text{,}\) then \(x\sim z\text{.}\)

Exploration 3.3.3.

Is the relation from Exploration 3.3.2 reflexive? Symmetric? Transitive?

Exploration 3.3.4.

Consider the relation \(\mathcal{B}\) defined on the set of all people by \((x,y)\in \mathcal{B}\) iff \(x\) and \(y\) have the same birthday. Is this relation reflexive? Symmetric? Transitive?

Exploration 3.3.5.

Let \(X = \set{1,2,3,4,5}\text{.}\)

  1. Define a relation \(\mathcal{R}\) on \(X\) that is reflexive, but not symmetric or transitive.
  2. Define a relation \(\mathcal{S}\) on \(X\) that is symmetric, but not reflexive or transitive.
  3. Define a relation \(\mathcal{T}\) on \(X\) that is transitive, but not symmetric or reflexive.
Equivalence Relation.

A relation that is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive is known as a equivalence relation.

Activity 3.3.6.

Which of the relations we've seen so far are equivalence relations? Can you think of any others?

Example 3.3.1.

Consider the relation \(\mathcal{P}\) on \(\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{P}\) is an equivalence relation.

Subsubsection 3.3.1.3 Partitions

Partitions.

Let \(X\) be a set. Then a partition \(\mathfrak{P}\) of \(X\) is a subsets of \(\pow(X)\) satisfying:

  1. Each element of \(\mathfrak{P}\) is nonempty
  2. If \(x\in X\text{,}\) then there is some \(S\in \mathfrak{P}\) with \(x\in S\)
  3. The family \(\mathfrak{P}\) is pairwise disjoint: if \(S,T\in \mathfrak{P}\text{,}\) then \(S\cap T = \emptyset\)

Exploration 3.3.7.

Let \(U\) denote the set of all cities in the United States, and let \(I\) denote the set of all states in the United States. For each state \(i\text{,}\) let \(T_i\) denote the set of all cities within the state \(i\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{F} = \setof{T_i}{i\in I}\) and let \(\sim\) denote the relation associated to \(\mathcal{F}\text{.}\)

  1. Is \(\sim\) reflexive?
  2. Is \(\sim\) symmetric?
  3. Is \(\sim\) transitive?
  4. Is \(\mathcal{F}\) a partition of \(U\text{?}\)
