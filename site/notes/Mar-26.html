Section 11.3 Mar. 26: Direct Proofs

Motivating Questions.

Here are some questions we'll consider.

  1. What is a direct proof?
  2. What characteristics make for a good proof?

Subsection 11.3.1 Outline

Today's material comes from Section 3.2 of the book. You're encouraged to read it before coming to class.

Investigation 11.3.1.

Decide which of the following are valid proofs of the following statement:

If \(a b\) is an even number, then \(a\) or \(b\) is even.

  1. Suppose \(a\) and \(b\) are odd. That is, \(a=2k+1\) and \(b=2m+1\) for some integers \(k\) and \(m\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{align*} ab \amp =(2k+1)(2m+1)\\ \amp =4km+2k+2m+1\\ \amp =2(2km+k+m)+1\text{.} \end{align*}

    Therefore \(ab\) is odd.

  2. Assume that \(a\) or \(b\) is even - say it is \(a\) (the case where \(b\) is even will be identical). That is, \(a=2k\) for some integer \(k\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{align*} ab \amp =(2k)b\\ \amp =2(kb)\text{.} \end{align*}

    Thus \(ab\) is even.

  3. Suppose that \(ab\) is even but \(a\) and \(b\) are both odd. Namely, \(ab = 2n\text{,}\) \(a=2k+1\) and \(b=2j+1\) for some integers \(n\text{,}\) \(k\text{,}\) and \(j\text{.}\) Then

    \begin{align*} 2n \amp =(2k+1)(2j+1)\\ 2n \amp =4kj+2k+2j+1\\ n \amp = 2kj+k+j+\frac{1}{2}\text{.} \end{align*}

    But since \(2kj+k+j\) is an integer, this says that the integer \(n\) is equal to a non-integer, which is impossible.

  4. Let \(ab\) be an even number, say \(ab=2n\text{,}\) and \(a\) be an odd number, say \(a=2k+1\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} ab \amp =(2k+1)b\\ 2n \amp =2kb+b\\ 2n-2kb\amp =b\\ 2(n-kb)\amp =b\text{.} \end{align*}

    Therefore \(b\) must be even.

There are three main styles of proof we'll discuss (aside from combinatorial and inductive proofs, which we've already looked at): direct proofs, proofs by contradiction, and proofs by contrapositive. Direct proofs are the most common, while proofs by contradiction are perhaps the most fun, and proofs by contrapositive feel a bit like magic. There are other special techniques as well, and we'll discuss them as we encounter examples of them.

Before looking at some examples, let's consider two definitions.

Definition 11.3.1.

Let \(a,b\in\Z\text{.}\) We say \(a\) divides \(b\text{,}\) written \(a | b\text{,}\) if there exist \(c\in\Z\) such that \(ac = b\text{.}\) In this case, we say that \(b\) is a multiple of \(a\) and that \(b\) is divisible by \(a\text{.}\)

Definition 11.3.2.

We say an integer \(m\in\Z\) is even if \(m\) is divisible by 2, i.e., there exists \(k\in\Z\) such that \(m = 2k\text{.}\) We say an integer \(n\in\Z\) is odd if \(n\) is not divisible by 2, i.e., there exists \(j\in\Z\) such that \(n = 2j+1\text{.}\)

Example 11.3.3.

Prove: For all integers \(n\text{,}\) if \(n\) is even, then \(n^2\) is even.

Solution

The format of the proof will be this: Let \(n\) be an arbitrary integer. Assume that \(n\) is even. Explain explain explain. Therefore \(n^2\) is even.

To fill in the details, we will basically just explain what it means for \(n\) to be even, and then see what that means for \(n^2\text{.}\) Here is a complete proof.

Exploration 11.3.2.

Write clear proofs of each of the following statements. Then swap yours with a friend's and offer feedback on their correctness and clarity.

  1. For all integers \(n\text{,}\) if \(n\) is odd, then \(n^2\) is odd.
  2. For all odd integers \(m,n\text{,}\) \(m+n\) is even.
  3. Let \(x\) be an integer. Then \(x\) is odd if and only if there exists an integer \(\ell\) for which \(x = 2\ell -1\text{.}\)
  4. Let \(a,b,c\in\Z\) with \(a|b\) and \(a|c\text{.}\) Then \(a|(b+c)\text{.}\)
  5. Let \(a,b,c\in\Z\) with \(a|b\) and \(a|c\text{.}\) Then \(a|bc\text{.}\)
  6. Let \(a,b,c\in\Z\) with \(a|b\) and \(b|c\text{.}\) Then \(a|c\text{.}\)
Activity 11.3.3.

Evaluate the following proofs.

  • If a proposition is false, the proposed proof, is, of course, incorrect. In this situation, find the error in the proof and then provide a counterexample showing that the proposition is false.
  • If a proposition is true, the proposed proof may still be incorrect. In this case, you are to determine why the proof is incorrect and then write a correct proof using the writing guidelines that have been presented in this book.
  • If a proposition is true and the proof is correct, you are to decide if the proof is well written or not. If it is well written, then you simply must indicate that this is an excellent proof and needs no revision. On the other hand, if the proof is not well written, then you must then revise the proof by writing it according to the guidelines presented in this text.

  1. If \(m\) is an even integer, then \(5m+4\) is an even integer.

    Solution

    We see that \(5m+4 = 10n +4 = 2(5n+2)\text{.}\) Therefore \(5m+4\) is an even integer.

  2. For all integers \(a,b,c\text{,}\) if \(a|bc\text{,}\) then \(a|b\) or \(a|c\text{.}\)

    Solution

    We assume that \(a,b,c\in\Z\) and that \(a|bc\text{.}\) Then there is an integer \(k\) such that \(ak=bc\text{.}\) We now factor \(k\) as \(k=mn\text{,}\) where \(m,n\) are integers. We then see that

    \begin{equation*} bc = mna. \end{equation*}

    This means that \(b=ma\) or \(c=na\) and hence, \(a|b\) or \(a|c\text{.}\)
